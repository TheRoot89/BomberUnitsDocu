16 About the game
17 --------------
18 So, **Bomberman** is a classic game type (Google!). We first want to replicate its classic, simple scheme of
19 moving your player figure, lay bombs, collect PowerUps and finally explode your opponents.
20 
21 As this is vitally important today, we also want to support **mixed multiplayer** (local, online).
22 Of course a game does not just start directly with a level and your fellow players already there.
23 There will have to be a menu UI, (online) player management, level selection, game-over screen etc...
24 
25 When this runs stable, we want to add our own take on the genre and add our **own features**. This even more
26 so than the above points is a group effort, where we can brainstorm, decide, implement and test together.
27 
28 Interested? Read the **\subpage page_getting_started** page so set things up.
29 For more details on what there is to do, read on :)

50 ### Game Design
51 The core of any project: figuring out what the f*** we actually want. In order to get going, we need at least
52 some basic ideas, preferably with an interesting twist. Then we need to refine these ideas to answer questions
53 such as:
54 
55 * How will playing this be fun?
56 * What are the core elements and themes?
57 * What shall the Player's experience be like?
58 * What should the graphical style be like? What that of the UI?
59 * What main functionality will the SW have to provide?
60 * What other, crazy ideas do we have? Do they fit in?
61 
62 The **tools to support this** are up to discussion - I just started with plain Powerpoint.
63 
64 ### Planning & Teamwork
65 So, imagine we have quite a big list of tasks, big and small, crazy ideas and stuff we just want to try to
66 see whether they work. We need to:
67 
68 1. Decide what has to be done in which order from a technical perspective.
69 2. What we want to do first of the things that require no special order. What are the ratios of gain vs.
70  effort for the features we want to add?
71 3. Who wants to do what? Is everyone happy with what he is doing and where we are going?
72  Does anyone need help?
73 
74 Also, stuff needs to be taken care of:
75 
76 * When and how do we meet?
77 * How do we want to collaborate? Are there any rules?
78 * Most importantly: How can we improve?
79 
80 One **tool to support this** is surely the project tab and issue tracking of
81 [GitHub](https://github.com/TheRoot89/BomberUnits), where our code is hosted anyway. It offers a way to
82 collect, sort and prioritize ideas as notes, track tasks, and rant about them in strong language.
83 Information not related to ideas or tasks may be tracked using these nice Markdown pages.

85 ### Art Design & Modelling
86 If this is not a text adventure, we will need to show some art, be it 2D or 3D. How can we achieve this
87 without hiring Weta Workshop? How do we achieve a reasonable look with comparably low effort?
88 
89 The **tools to support this** are manyfold, with many specialized solutions for the different artsy tasks,
90 depending on what we want. Simple models can be made for example with [AssetForge](https://assetforge.io/),
91 while arbitrarily complex stuff can be done with Blender. Maps and textures can be found, also free, all over
92 the web. The final look will also largely depend on the Postprocessing Stack, allowing cool looks for low-poly
93 models.
94 
95 ### Rigging & Animation
96 Rigging is the task of providing a virtual skeleton, or *rig*, for your models. This allows to deform the
97 models to assume natural looking poses. A sequence of such poses forms an Animation, which is a kind of asset
98 importable and replay-able in Unity.
99 
100 **Tools to support this:** Rigging is typically supported by modeling SW, such as Blender.
101 Animations may be done in the Unity editor as well, while the final chaining of animations with animation
102 state machines is a task of game development, also supported graphically by the Unity Editor.
103 
104 ### Level Design
105 This is what the title implies, and you have probably seen good and bad levels abound yourselves. It is a bit
106 of a cross-over task:<br>
107 The design aspect of this is a part of the *Game Design*, especially when thinking of cool level features,
108 which will need support from a *Scripting* perspective.
109 
110 For the actual modeling of a level Scene, using
111 mainly the Unity Editor as **Tool**, you will combine Models as well as maybe create your own stuff, as a
112 *Modeling* guy would do. Poor fella.
113 
114 ### Scripting
115 This is the art of learning what the Unity API has to offer, how to use that to design an understandable and
116 reusable SW architecture and - of course - coding the thing in C# (or Java Script alternatively). The scripts
117 bring the game objects to life, let you play with friends, produce tons of log output, and so on...<br>
118 Game development may sound like it is all about Coding/Scripting, but as you see, there are many daunting
119 tasks each worthy ofa whole profession in the industry.
120 
121 The **tools to do that** are Unity and a supported IDE, such as Visual Studio or Visual Studio Code. Have a
122 look at the Tooling section (\ref page_tooling) for how to set those up. There is no black magic involved, as
123 Unity pretty much has the whole toolchain, up to deployment, built-in. Nice.
124 
125 ### Effects
126 This category wraps up some of the typical features found in Game Engines: particle systems, sound effects and
127 the relevant scripting making effects *affecting* the game world. This is also a bit of a crossover task, and
128 you get to play with explosions. Also in the effects category are screen effects, supported by the rendering
129 pipeline used, which is quite the topic of its own.
130 
131 The Unity editor may be all the **tool** you need to get started. Also Unity gives away quite a lot of
132 free-to-use effects we can start with. Building on that, you may need to roam the Net and use some tools of
133 the other disciplines to create you very own uber-effects!
134 
135 ### Post processing
136 In Post processing, meaning after the basic rendering of the frame, it is possible to apply further
137 transformations to apply effect or improve style and image quality. Typical tasks are Anti-Aliasing or the
138 Bloom effect. This has to be designed, tuned and tested.
139 From the **tooling** perspective, all this should be supported by Unity itself I think - but I am by no means
140 experienced in that area.
141 
142 ### DevOps
143 If we decide to have, e.g. build automation, common tool configurations (such as for Doxygen in the doc
144 folder), etc. - someone will have to look after that. Someone willing to be the all-important enabler of the
145 whole team. That someone could be you!
146 
147 The skills learned/needed here are probably no special to game development, and the **tools** are manyfold,
148 depending on what the team deems worthy automating.
149 
150 ### Testing
151 No time for the other stuff? No worries - we need people that from time to time have a look at the current
152 state and tell us what we f**** up. At pat on the back is also helpful sometimes.
153 
154 And then, there is *proper* testing, including *Unit Tests*. It may not seem reasonable for the dynamic nature
155 of game scripting code, but it is very well possible and supported by Unity's own set own **tools**.
156 The scripters will kiss the feet of the guy who tells them how to use those tools. At the very latest when
157 complexity increases and devs become afraid of breaking things accidentally.
