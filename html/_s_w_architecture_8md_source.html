18 On Unity SW artifacts in general
19 -------------------------------------
20 
21 The image below shows my simplified understanding of the types of SW artifacts you come across while scripting
22 for Unity:
23 
24 ![Unity SW artifact hierarchy](/doxy_in/images/SWArtifacts.svg)
25 
26 Each SW Component making up the program may make use of the building blocks above. Starting from the top:
27 
28 * **Game Objects** are the virtual objects present in any scene of the game. They may not even be visible, but
29 are the central means of invoking scripts by attaching so-called Behaviours to them. Game Objects (GOs) can
30 be made a Prefab asset, which means their current composition and values are saved to disc as a kind of
31 template.
32 * **MonoBehaviours** are, as mentioned, the base class of scripts attachable to GOs. They offer a range of
33 Callbacks to be implemented, such as the Update() function, which is called for every active MonoBehaviour on
34 every object each frame. Attached to GOs, scripts are called "Components".
35 * **StateMachineBehaviours** are not attached to objects, but to the states of a state machine, which can be
36 visually designed within the Unity Editor. This allows to compose and visualize state-full behaviour easily.
37 The state machine instance itself lives, again, as a Component on a GO.
38 * **Classes and Data Structures** are your usual C# object-oriented SW. They are at some point used Behaviours
39 to allocate functionality nicely without having the overhead of making everything a Behaviour subclass.
40 Also Behaviours are instantiated by the engine and have no own constructors, while your own classes of course
41 can have those.
42 * **Scriptable Object (Instances)** are your own subclasses of the "ScriptableObject" class provided by the
43 engine. You can create and save instances of these classes to disc during build time. The can serve as data
44 containers which can be drag-n-dropped onto behaviours for reference. This also allows usage as a means of
45 communication between components.
46 * **Helpers & Editor Extensions** are your custom C# helpers, like extension classes, as well as various
47 capabilities to extend the Unity Editor with own scripts! This encompasses custom menu entries or custom
48 views for your components in the Unity Inspector. Editor extensions have to be placed in "Editor" folders in
49 the asset hierarchy.
50 * **Engine Features** are used across all levels. Most prominent is the Unity Inspector feature, which allows
51 setting member variable values for provided and custom components and ScriptableObject instances from Unity
52 directly.<br>
53 These member variables are "serializable", i.e. Unity knows how to save and read their values to disc to
54 provide the above capabilities. Note that all serialized members are instantiated by the engine, e.g. when the
55 owning object is created. Constructors are not used this way.<br>
56 There are of course many more engine features, such as Mecanim-based state-machines, which shall be mentioned
57 where they are used.
